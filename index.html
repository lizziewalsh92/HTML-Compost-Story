Where Will It All Go?

Across New York City, Composting Is Now Mandatory—But Are There Enough Sites To Handle New York’s Food Waste?

As of October 6, 2024, residents across the five boroughs will be required to separate their food waste from trash, but data shows that some Manhattan zip codes may not have enough bins for their residents

By: Lizzie Walsh

November 18, 2024

The onus is on property owners to provide composting bins to their tenants, but New Yorkers already only recycle about 17% of their total waste, so expecting each property owner to provide these compost bins is unlikely. Thankfully, the city has a number of public compost drop-off sites where tenants can compost while they wait for their residential bin.

Comparing the number of compost drop-off sites in each Manhattan zip code to the estimated 2024 population of that zip code, I found drastic differences in compost site availability across the borough.

Some neighborhoods only have one drop-off site in their zip code, while others have 250 sites—but this doesn’t always correlate to population size. If the city wants composting to happen across the five boroughs, they’ll need to provide more public drop-off sites tailored to population size.

Charlie Bayrer, co-founder of the largest community compost processing system in New York City, EarthMatterNY, says "Since the roll-out of curbside is complete city-wide, DSNY said there will not be further expansion of [smart bins or community drop-off sites]. Looking at this data, the population numbers seem very low."
